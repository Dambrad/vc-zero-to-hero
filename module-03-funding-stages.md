### 3.3 â€” Investment Instruments: How Deals Are Structured

When an investor gives you money, they need a document that says what they get in return. The three main instruments are:

---

#### Instrument 1: SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)
*Created by Y Combinator in 2013. Now the dominant early-stage instrument.*

A SAFE is **not a loan** and **not equity yet**. It is a promise: "We give you money now, and in return we get the right to convert that into equity at a future priced round."

**Key terms in a SAFE:**

**Valuation Cap:** The maximum valuation at which the SAFE converts to equity.
> Example: You raise a SAFE with a $5M cap. Later, you raise a Series A at a $20M pre-money valuation. The SAFE holder converts as if the company was worth only $5M â€” so they get a better price per share than new investors.

**Discount Rate:** SAFE holders often also get a percentage discount on the price paid by new investors.
> Example: 20% discount means if Series A investors pay $1.00/share, SAFE holders pay only $0.80/share.

**MFN (Most Favored Nation):** Safeguards the investor â€” if you offer better terms on a future SAFE, they get those terms too.

**Why founders love SAFEs:**
- Fast and cheap to execute (a few days, minimal legal fees)
- No interest accruing
- No fixed repayment deadline
- Simpler than a priced round

**Why investors are okay with SAFEs:**
- They get cap protection (the cap limits how much the company can be worth when they convert)
- Widely accepted and standardized

> ğŸ“ **Template:** Y Combinator publishes free, standard SAFE documents at [ycombinator.com/documents](https://www.ycombinator.com/documents)

---

#### Instrument 2: Convertible Note
*The predecessor to the SAFE. Still widely used.*

A convertible note is a **loan** that converts into equity at a future event (usually the next priced round). Because it's a loan, it has:

- **Principal:** The amount borrowed
- **Interest Rate:** Typically 4â€“8% annually
- **Maturity Date:** The date the loan must convert or be repaid (usually 12â€“24 months)
- **Conversion terms:** Valuation cap and/or discount rate (same as SAFE)

**Key difference from SAFE:** The note can technically be called due at maturity if you haven't raised a new round. This gives investors leverage that SAFEs don't have.

**When are convertible notes still used?**
- When the investor specifically prefers notes (some international investors)
- When there are legal or tax reasons to prefer debt instruments
- In some geographies where SAFEs are less common

---

#### Instrument 3: Priced Round (Preferred Stock)
*The full deal. Used for Series A and beyond.*

In a priced round, the company and investors **agree on a valuation today**, and the investor receives **Preferred Stock** â€” a class of shares with special rights and protections.

A priced round requires:
- A formal term sheet
- Legal due diligence
- Certificate of Incorporation amendment
- Investor Rights Agreement
- Right of First Refusal and Co-Sale Agreement
- Voting Agreement

**Why preferred?** Preferred stock comes with special rights that common stock (what founders get) doesn't have. These include:
- **Liquidation preference** (they get paid back first in an exit)
- **Anti-dilution protection** (see Module 05)
- **Board representation**
- **Information rights** (access to financials)

### 3.4 â€” What Goes Into a Pitch Deck?

Raising capital requires convincing investors. The pitch deck is your primary sales tool.

**The 10-slide framework (from Sequoia's guide):**

| Slide | What It Covers |
|-------|---------------|
| 1. Problem | What urgent, painful problem are you solving? |
| 2. Solution | How do you solve it uniquely? |
| 3. Why Now | Why is this the right moment? |
| 4. Market Size | TAM, SAM, SOM (see below) |
| 5. Product | Show the thing (screenshots, demo, video) |
| 6. Business Model | How do you make money? |
| 7. Traction | Growth metrics, revenue, user numbers |
| 8. Team | Who are you and why are you the ones to win? |
| 9. Competition | Honest competitive landscape |
| 10. The Ask | How much are you raising and what will you do with it? |

**Market sizing terms explained:**
- **TAM (Total Addressable Market):** The total market if you had 100% share globally
- **SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market):** The portion you could realistically reach
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):** What you can realistically capture in the next few years

> ğŸ’¡ **Common pitch deck mistakes:**
> - Slide after slide of text â€” use visuals
> - "No competition" slides â€” every investor knows this is false
> - Vague financials â€” show you understand your numbers
> - Missing the ask â€” tell them how much you want and why

## ğŸŒ Real-World Case Study: Stripe's Fundraising Journey

**Stripe** is now one of the most valuable private companies in the world (valued at ~$50B+ in recent rounds). But it started like every other startup.

**2009:** Patrick and John Collison started building Stripe in their college dorms. The idea: make it radically simple for developers to accept payments online. At the time, payment processing was a nightmare.

**2010 â€” First Funding:** Stripe was accepted into Y Combinator (Summer 2010 batch). YC invested roughly $20K for approximately 7% (standard at the time). Pre-money implied value: under $300K.

**2011 â€” Seed Round:** After YC, they raised a seed round led by Peter Thiel, Sequoia, and a16z â€” raising $2M at a roughly $20M valuation. This was unusually high for seed at the time, reflecting the pedigree of the founders and the early evidence of developer adoption.

**2012 â€” Series A:** $18M raised at a $100M valuation. By now, Stripe had significant developer adoption and revenue growing rapidly.

**Why Stripe Could Raise at These Terms:**
- Founders were ex-MIT and ex-Harvard with strong technical credibility
- YC network created instant credibility signal
- The product solved a real, painful problem
- Developer-led growth was measurable and compounding

**The Instrument Choice:**
Early rounds used convertible notes. By Series A, they moved to priced preferred stock rounds â€” as is standard once the business and valuation are more established.
